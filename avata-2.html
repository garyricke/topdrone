Chapter 01 — History

From Tesla's Boat
to the Cinewhoop

The Avata 2 didn't appear from nowhere. It is the result of 125 years of human obsession with putting cameras into impossible places. Understanding that history makes every flight feel intentional.

1898
Nikola Tesla — The First Remote Vehicle
Tesla demonstrated a radio-controlled boat in New York, shocking audiences who suspected hidden operators inside. The concept of controlling a vehicle beyond physical contact was born.
1944
BQ-7 "Aphrodite" — First FPV Aircraft
The US Army Air Forces converted B-17 bombers into remote explosives carriers with cockpit cameras. A pilot in a trailing aircraft guided the drone via live television feed — the first true FPV system.
1989
Project Cyclops — The Hobbyist Era Begins
Carl Berry mounted bulky security cameras on RC planes. By the early 1990s he had built custom arcade-style simulators to fly them — proving immersive FPV was possible outside government labs.
2014
Airgonay Video — FPV Goes Viral
A European enthusiast group posted a video of drones racing through dense forest at high speed. The video catalyzed the formation of the Drone Racing League and MultiGP, turning a niche hobby into a sport.
2017
"Muscle Up" — The Cinewhoop is Born
Robert McIntosh's short film proved that ducted-propeller drones could achieve cinema-quality footage in tight spaces. DJI took notice. The Avata series followed.

Using Manual Mode

Safety Warning Manual mode disables all flight assistance. The aircraft has no automatic stabilization and can reach any attitude. Failure to operate properly is a safety risk and may cause the aircraft to crash. Manual mode is disabled by default and requires explicit activation in the Goggles 3 menu. Only experienced pilots should use Manual mode. Practice extensively in a flight simulator before flying real hardware.

The RC3 supports flight simulators including Liftoff, Uncrashed, Drone Racing League (DRL), and Drone Champions League (DCL). Spend at minimum several weeks in simulator before attempting Manual mode outdoors.

Enabling Manual Mode — Step by Step:

1Turn the RC3 over. Open the rubber grip behind the throttle stick.
2Using the included 1.5mm hex key, tighten F1 (resistance) and F2 (spring) adjustment screws on the left stick so the throttle stick no longer auto-recenters.
3Power on the aircraft, Goggles 3, and RC3. Make sure all devices are linked.
4In the goggles, press the 5D button and open the menu. Go to Settings → Control → Remote Controller → Button Customization → Custom Mode → Set to Manual Mode.
5Flip the flight mode switch to M. Note: first-time Manual mode has an attitude restriction enabled. Once comfortable, this can be disabled in the goggles, and Gain and Expo can be adjusted.

Starting Motors in Manual Mode (CSC):

1Hold the throttle stick in the lowest position.
2Press the Start/Stop button twice rapidly. Motors will spin up.
3Push the throttle stick up slowly and smoothly to take off.
4To stop motors after landing, press the Start/Stop button twice again.
Tip When in Manual mode, pressing the RTH/Flight Pause button once makes the aircraft brake and hover, and automatically switches the flight mode back to Normal mode. It is recommended to switch to Normal mode before landing.

Chapter 06 — Filters

ND, CPL & UV —
The 180° Rule

Cinematic motion blur is not an accident. It is a deliberate technical choice. Because the Avata 2 has no adjustable aperture, ND filters are your only tool for controlling shutter speed — and shutter speed controls how the world moves in your footage.

The 180-Degree Rule Set your shutter speed to double the frame rate. Filming at 60fps? Use 1/120 shutter. In bright daylight, this would blow out the image — so you add an ND filter to cut incoming light and restore proper exposure while keeping the blur natural.
ND8

Chapter 08 — Training

The Path to
Mastery

The transition from a consumer camera drone to an FPV platform is steep. Navigate it systematically. Skipping steps doesn't save time — it destroys equipment.

01
Simulation First
The RC3 is compatible with Liftoff, Uncrashed, and the DRL simulator. Log at least 20 hours in simulation before attempting Manual mode outdoors.
  • Throttle control — maintain altitude in a narrow hallway
  • Coordinated turns — roll and yaw together for cinematic flow
  • Emergency recovery — build the panic response that saves your drone
02
Real-World Drills
Open field, no obstacles. Build the muscle memory that simulation started. Feel the real-world inertia and ground effect before you add complexity.
  • The Orbit — 360° circle around a fixed subject
  • Figure-8s — master inertia through directional changes
  • Low-altitude proximity — get comfortable with ground effect over grass
03
Cinematic Techniques
Flight proficiency is the prerequisite. Storytelling is the destination. These are the maneuvers that transform technical skill into visual narrative.
  • Reveal shots — emerge from behind obstacles dramatically
  • Dives and Split-S — use verticality for cinematic transitions
  • Subject chasing — follow moving subjects at consistent distance
